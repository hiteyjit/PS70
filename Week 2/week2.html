Week 2: Getting Used to 3D Designing

This week's focus was on understanding the fundamentals of using Fusion 360 & making our very own box to store our projects in.

Box-Making

I had a few designs ideas for my box. We had been shown some basic no-lid box ideas in class, and those seemed great, but I wanted something more unique for my project. So, I came up with the idea of making a nice large box with 2 rows of 3 drawers.

In the process to ideate, I thought that I would use parts of the useless box that we had designed in class, as this was a simple box with an open top and 4 sides that could be used as a drawer. (insert image of useless box here)

To make the sections for the shelves to go in the box onto which the drawers would sit, I thought that I could make areas on the walls of the box that would be scored by the laser cutter so there is a 'ridge' for cardboard pieces to sit on easily: (insert image here)

HOWEVER, after spending substantial time on this idea, I pivoted massively to try and make a box more reflective of my project. This made me want to make a: SKATEBOARD SHAPED BOX!

The inspiration

This was the initial inspiration for the shape of my box: a boosted board. This seemed like it was long and wide enough (compared to a regular skateboard) to fit most of my stuff. To be honest, it also looked better as a 2D shape than a regular skateboard.

I then imported this same image into Fusion 360 as a canvas and then traced out the basic shape of the skateboard using the sketch tool. Once I had done this, I used the 'offset' tool to make a border around the base that would act as the walls of the box. Once making the sketches, I exxtruded everything to make it look like this:

However, as you can see, the one thing that made it look good was the one thing that made it impossible to make: the curvature. After an extensive discussion with Bobby, I realized that the Laser Cutter would not be able to create the curvature of the walls I would need to have it wrap around the base well. This makes sense, since the laser cutter can only print 2D shapes with uniform thickness & straight shapes. We tried many features in Fusion, such as splitting the body, trying to make it into a flat 'sheet metal' body, etc, but none of these worked and it was simply getting too complicated to figure out. We could have cut a simple large rectangle and have uniform rasterized cuts on the surface to make it easy to simply bend, but it would have taken too much effort to glue it together.

As a result, I completely uprooted my design and instead of having curved bases, I instead made the entire design mostly out of rectangles. I created entirely new parameters (below), and made a rectangular base that looked somewhat like a skateboard:

After this, I followed a youtube tutorial on trying to add 'teeth' to the design so I could make the walls easily slot onto the base:

I also wanted to have some cool designs on the sides of the box. The idea I had was to have my name on one side of the box, and the other side have the Harvard Shield on it. The name was simple enough, all I had to do was create a new sketch, add a text box, and modify it to my liking:

The Veritas Shield, however, was not so simple. I could not simply trace out all the elements from the shield myself as that would be extremely time consuming. As a solution to the complication of tracing, I found a method to convert a PDF into a usable sketch in Fusion.

This involved finding a PDF of the shield and importing it into AutoCAD. Once it imports, it is able to trace out the features of the PDF (provided it is not a mapping of a pixel photo), and then lets you export a DXF file of the design:

Once I created the DXF file, I imported it into my Fusion 360 design, which then allowed me to move it around, scale it, and adjust it until it reached a point of my liking:

So, by largely using the sketch tool, I was able to create a good looking design of the skateboard box:

The walls:

I also added these small skateboard-style handles to the sides of the box that were a nice detail I thought was fitting for the entire project:

All I had to do now was to create a new sketch, create projections of each side individually in each sketch, and then export each sketch as a DXF that contained the designs. You can also download these files to see for yourself.

Now, it was simply a matter of making sure the laser cutter processed the design correctly, and I was able to cut out the walls for my box!

Once cut, all I had to do was join the walls together at the teeth linkages (I used hot glue), let it dry, and voila! My skateboard-shaped box was ready!
